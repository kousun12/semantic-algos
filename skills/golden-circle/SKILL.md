# Golden Circle

Use this skill when a topic would benefit from separating purpose, method, and output: Why, How, and What.

## Purpose

The Golden Circle frames communication and strategy in three layers:

- **Why**: purpose, belief, motivation, or reason it matters.
- **How**: method, approach, differentiator, operating principles, or process.
- **What**: concrete product, action, deliverable, feature, or observable behavior.

It is especially useful for pitches, product strategy, personal motivation, organizational alignment, writing introductions, and diagnosing whether a plan has a clear purpose.

## Procedure

1. Identify the topic, decision, product, or message.
2. Ask: Why does this matter? What belief, need, problem, or purpose drives it?
3. Ask: How is the purpose pursued? What approach or principles make it distinct?
4. Ask: What is actually being done, built, offered, or changed?
5. Check alignment: does the What actually express the How and Why?
6. If useful, rewrite the message from the inside out: Why → How → What.

## Output format

Use this concise structure unless the user asks otherwise:

```markdown
## Why
...

## How
...

## What
...

## Alignment check
...

## Inside-out version
...
```

## Guardrails

- Do not invent a lofty Why when the real purpose is pragmatic; practical purposes are valid.
- Keep the What concrete.
- Distinguish motivation from marketing language.
- If the Why, How, and What are misaligned, say so directly and propose a tighter version.

## Canonical questions

Best for pitches, positioning, and alignment checks:

- "Help me write the intro slide for my startup pitch."
- "Our product page lists 14 features — what should it actually say?"
- "Why am I actually doing this side project?"
- "Rewrite this job posting so good candidates care."
- "Does our roadmap match our stated mission?"

Poor fits: causal analysis ("why did X fail?" — use n-whys), and problems with no audience or purpose dimension.
